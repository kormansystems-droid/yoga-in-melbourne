Janita, on touch as its own language

Janita Doelken teaches with her hands as much as her words. Across years and continents she has gathered practices, Thai massage, ZenThai Shiatsu, the fire of yang yoga and the stillness of yin, and knitted them into a single, touch-infused method of teaching. And increasingly, deliberately, that practice has come to rest in one place: Yin.

Today Janita teaches at Grass Roots Yoga, St Kilda, and Within, the South Yarra studio she also manages, alongside private classes, massage practice, and a Yin teacher training course she co-leads. But the range she draws on took a long time, and a lot of learnings, to gather.

Coming to the mat

Yoga began at the edges of a life full on sport. "I was always running, playing volleyball, doing Yang sports," she remembers; yoga was an occasional counterweight, once a month at most. Then a volleyball injury forced her to stop and, harder still, to rest. Gentle yoga, prescribed by a physio, felt at first "almost like a punishment." It didn't stay that way. "I quickly learned it was doing magic to my body." She began practising more, then daily.

For a while it stayed physical, "the asana was what brought me to the mat", a grounding ritual which persisted through years of corporate work, an occupation she "didn't really feel at home in."

It was teacher training, taken to understand rather than to teach, that changed what yoga was for her. "I realised it was so much more than the asana. I had this moment of crying in the middle of a shape, not knowing where it came from, a moment of release, and real understanding of myself."

The gathering

Through her healing from injury, Janita had benefited from osteopathy and remedial massage, and came away fascinated by how the body heals under skilled hands. She trained in Germany, then in Thailand, where a Thai massage course left a lasting mark. "It was so profound, understanding how just a gentle pressure in certain points can access so much information about a person."

Back in Melbourne she kept building. Training in ZenThai Shiatsu, a practice woven from Thai massage, osteopathy and shiatsu, grounded in the meridian lines of Traditional Chinese medicine, she met the teacher who would become her partner, Alessia Frisina. ZenThai works the way Yin does, from the inner yin lines outward to the yang, addressing the body "almost like a dance."

All of it, she says, has knitted together. "It's not just hands on a body. It's really tuning into everyone's individual anatomy, how they move, how they respond." That's what she brings to her classes now, paired with touch as its own language. In Yin especially, it isn't correction. "It's not about correcting a shape, not at all. It's about enhancing the student's experience", offered gently, never breaking the stillness.

Into Yin

She still teaches yang, and loves it. But her focus has settled, unmistakably, into Yin. "Yin is my thing," she says, not something she anticipated, but something she keeps choosing, because she sees the need. "So many people don't even know how much they'd benefit from it." Yin is where she feels she can meet a student most fully: "help them ground, and really let go, even if it's just for the hour they're in class."

"Yin is my thing."

After a couple of years teaching it, she wanted to go further. She was offered a space at Grass Roots Yoga, and asked Alessia to partner with her. Together they built The Yin Path teacher training. "It's a path. It always will be, because it's an ongoing practice. You can step off it for a moment, but you can find it again." Their second annual training runs this August. They work well as a pair, she says, because they balance: "we have both strengths and weaknesses, and we beautifully balance them out."

Coming home

Ask Jani what grounds her and the answer is immediate: nature. "Probably my number one value in life is to be out in nature, and just breathe the fresh air." She's wary of how the city pulls us under. "We get lost in the busyness, nature is really what grounds us. Just walking barefoot for five minutes will ground you." When meditating, she looks for it, a glance out the window before she closes her eyes. "It's proof, to me. We're looking for it. We need it, a beautiful, simple cure to the stresses of day-to-day life."

Yoga itself has been a kind of home. Not originally from Australia, and a born traveller, she found something portable in it: "you roll out the mat and practise wherever you are, coming back to yourself, no matter where you are." Years in, she feels the difference in herself. "I'm a much calmer version of myself. More compassionate. A more patient mother." She sees it in her students who keep returning, too. "We all say these things, patience, presence, compassion. But there's really something to it. There's magic behind it, and I can feel it."

It's the same instinct, in the end, that turned a reluctant rest into a calling: a belief that the body already knows the way back, and that a pair of attentive hands, and moments of stillness, can help it remember.
