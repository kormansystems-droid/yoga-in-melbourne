ZoeKanat

Guiding people into deep rest, through Yoga Nidra, meditation, and the conviction that rest is medicine for the modern world.

Guiding rest, worldwide
"I came out of it and my mind was blown. Where did I go? I felt this deep sense of peace, but also clarity."
Zoe Kanat, on her first Yoga Nidra

Zoe Kanat guides people into rest. Through Yoga Nidra, meditation and the quiet discipline of slowing down, she has built a following of some 33,000 people on the meditation app Insight Timer, runs her own online courses, and teaches from a single, unmistakable conviction: that rest is not a luxury, it is a medicine for modern times.

But long before she guided anyone into stillness, Zoe was already doing the deeper work beneath it: holding space for people in the hardest chapters of their lives.

Drawn to the hard chapters

She began, after school, studying teaching, pulled by a sense that she was good with people and wanted to help them. Formal teaching didn't perfectly match her expectations, but the instinct beneath it did. She volunteered at Sacred Heart Mission, serving tea and coffee and sitting with people who needed company. At the Lighthouse Foundation she worked with kids from difficult backgrounds, taking them on excursions, helping them clock up the driving hours for their licence. Then came a role with Victoria's human services, supporting young people who'd offended: picking them up, driving them to their community work, taking them for lunch and a chat.

Their worlds were often marked by domestic violence and drugs, and it was, she says, "a real eye-opener." It was also clarifying. "I realised I was good in those environments." The vocation was there long before it was fully recognised.

The physical years

Yoga arrived, at first, as something else entirely. Her mother had practised, and in her early twenties Zoe began going herself, drawn, like so many, to the physicality. "I was really into trying to achieve all these poses, headstands and handstands, all the flexibility things. I didn't really know the depth of yoga."

It was a different era of practice: classes ran for two hours, the teacher's presence and guidance being central. Her first teacher was Eugenie Knox, whose Fitzroy studio, Dance of Life, had a pot-belly stove you'd gather around before popping fifteen dollars in a box and beginning. Slow, demonstrated, unhurried. It planted something, even if Zoe didn't yet know what.

The turn inward

Then came children, and, after both of her births, postnatal depression. Her recovery is what turned her toward stillness in earnest: first mindfulness, learned from a therapist, then formal study in psychotherapy and counselling. Only once she had come through it herself did she go back to help others through the same, volunteering with PANDA, the Post and Antenatal Depression Association, training as a counsellor, and taking calls from parents walking the road she had just walked. The carer had become, quietly, someone who could hold others precisely because she had been there.

It was in that psychotherapy training that she first glimpsed her gift. Asked to lead the group through a body-scan relaxation, her instructor's assessment was poignant. "She said she found it really hard to grade me, that I had a real gift." It was, she thought, something in her voice, her pacing. It had put her into a type of trance state.

Where did I go?

The path deepened through insight meditation. Zoe found the Melbourne Insight Meditation community and its teacher Jess Huon, and gave herself over to years of courses and silent retreats, long enough to sense that this "was going to support everything in my life." On one of those retreats, after lunch, the teachers had everyone lie down and led them, without naming it, through Yoga Nidra.

"The first time, I came out of it and my mind was blown. I was just, wow, where did I go?" What she felt was peace, and beneath it something sharper: clarity. "That's where it all began."

Rest as healing

From student, she became teacher. An old friend, a co-founder of Happy Melon Studios, invited her to train together and lead its meditation offering. The two studied at the Gita School of Yoga in Collingwood, one of the oldest in Melbourne. Then, after hearing American author Karen Brody describe Daring to Rest, a programme built entirely around Yoga Nidra, Zoe felt an immediate pull, "that's for me." She trained with Brody, then mentored in her programme for some four years, and added further Yoga Nidra trainings on top. "It became my absolute love. I felt really at home in the whole process."

In 2016 she began uploading tracks to Insight Timer, back when it was free and unpaid; that library has since grown to the point where she supports some 33,000 followers on the platform. There is also a monthly live session, as well as her own courses. The latest, Gentle Mornings, runs fourteen consecutive days, a small daily practice, that offers consistency, the thing the drop-in world rarely offers. It grew from her own morning ritual. "It shapes my whole day, how I communicate, how I meet challenges. It makes me gentler with myself. It's kind of like a prayer to myself and my purpose in life."

Underneath all of it sits the conviction she now shares with her own teacher, Tracee Stanley. "Rest is not a luxury, it's our birthright." Real rest, she insists, is non-doing, a surrender against the pull of the phone, the screen, the endless reaching outward that severs us from our own intuition. We fall out of sync with our natural rhythms, and pay for it in the nights we can't sleep. Her mission is clearly stated: to normalise rest. Not only sleep, but pockets of the day, a cup of tea and nothing else, where we are simply, fully present.

Coming home

There's an importance to it for her, too, particular to this moment. "In these times, where so much is being taken from us by AI and technology, what we have is our humanity, our ability to find wisdom from within, rather than reaching outside ourselves for all the answers."

It is, in the end, where her whole life has been quietly heading: from the young people she drove to their community work, to parents she counselled through dark times, to the tens of thousands she now guides each night toward sleep, and to herself. A carer who spent decades holding space for others, and found, in rest, the way to finally come home.
