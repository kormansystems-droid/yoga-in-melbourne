Warrior One

Three studios, one heartbeat, the teachers. From a sixteen-mat room in Brighton to three thriving studios across bayside Melbourne, built class by class on the belief that nurturing a community matters as much as what happens on the mat.

＋ Follow Warrior One
"Ultimately it's the teaching team. They're the heartbeat of the studios, it's what they bring to the practice that keeps people coming."
Nova Rosaia, co-founder

Today Warrior One spans three studios, Brighton, Mordialloc and Mornington, with around forty teachers running some 140 classes a week. It's a lot of yoga for what co-founder Nova Rosaia affectionately calls "a big little business." But ask her what actually holds it together, and she doesn't reach for the numbers, she reaches for the people who teach. That conviction shapes how Warrior One hires: a deliberately thorough selection process, and a mentoring pathway for newer teachers, often graduates of the studio's own training, until they can "hold a room really well."

From sixteen mats

Nova's own path in began at twenty-five, in the sweat-soaked heat of Hot Box in Prahran, back when hot yoga was the thing. "I went to my first class and I was obsessed," she remembers, five years of near-daily practice before a love of yoga met a love of business, and she decided to merge the two.

The first Warrior One was humble: sixteen mats in a New Street, Brighton studio, on a plot that, remarkably, has been a yoga studio for over a hundred years. Building it meant building a community one class at a time. Mordialloc followed in year three, chosen for its two rooms, to allow teacher training and regular classes to run simultaneously. Then, as Brighton burst its sixteen-mat seams, the current, larger Brighton studio was leased. The space was going to be leased as an apartment until the owner downstairs thought he'd rather have yoga in the building.

Through the fire

That studio opened in late 2019, "very bad timing," Nova laughs, "three months later, COVID." Brand new, beautiful, and promptly shut. Necessity became invention: Warrior One's online studio was born in that empty Brighton room, teachers and cameras assembled each day until even that was no longer allowed. A Mornington studio, built during lockdown, opened on New Year's Day 2021 straight into another lockdown. For a stretch they were floating four studios at once, under the uncertain cloud of restrictions and closure.

They came through it. The smallest Brighton space closed in 2022, and the three studios that remain are, in Nova's words, thriving, with the memories of those days well and truly behind them.

Bridging the gap

What preoccupies Warrior One now is connection. Yoga, Nova points out, is intimate but strangely solitary: "you have this personal experience, and then you're off, people grab their phone, look down, and you've lost them." So the studio has leaned hard into community, free coffee-and-chat mornings across all three sites, members' events, and a milestone-gifting project of handmade presents for regulars (one member has now clocked up more than 1,700 classes).

The bigger connectors have been there for years: teacher trainings that build close connections, and an overseas retreat to Thailand that's run every year since 2015, taking many people away together. "People make lifelong friends," Nova says, "and then you see them in the studio." The free local events are an attempt to open that same door to everyone, a really low barrier to entry to the friendships a studio can hold.

And the future?

"I think there probably is another studio coming, maybe in another year or two," muses Nova. With a successful dynamic in place, it's clear that people love what Warrior One is bringing.
